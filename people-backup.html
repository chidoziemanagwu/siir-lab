Dr. Chukwuma "Chuma" Nnaji

Education: 
  • Ph.D., Civil Engineering, Oregon State University, 2018
  • MBA, Oregon State University, 2017
  • M.S., Civil Engineering, Oregon State University, 2015
  • B.S., Building, Imo State University, Owerri, Nigeria, 2010
Location: 263 Hardaway Hall ​
At the CII-Lab, we study various risks and hazards that are inherent to the construction environment. As part of the broad vision, we aim to always identify and investigate new and emerging high-impact, low-frequency (HILF) and high-frequency, low-impact (HFLI) accidents and injury trends in the construction workplace. Our ensuing research results provide evidence used to direct and inform the use of new and existing safety controls for intervention and prevention activities. We also monitor the overall impact of occupational health research. Currently, we are developing new surveillance methods, tools, and analytical techniques to help improve safety awareness (hazard identification and safety risk assessment, for instance), reduce worker fatigue and hearing loss, and enhance human-robot interactions.​

Ifeanyi Okpala

Education: 
  • M.S., Civil Engineering, The University of Alabama, 2019
  • B.Eng., Civil Engineering, The University of Agriculture, Abeokuta, Nigeria, 2015
Location: 113 Hardaway Hall ​
With the understanding of the need to curb high workers’ fatality rate in the construction industry, Ifeanyi is investigating effective methods of enhancing the utilization of emerging technologies for construction safety risk mitigation. This strides are taken on the backdrop of existing practicable knowledge on the potential impact of construction technologies in reducing workplace accidents. At present, he is developing a success model for implementing Wearable Sensing Devices in the construction industry.

Olugbenro Ogunrinde

Education: 
  • M.S., Construction Management, University of Lagos, Nigeria, 2013
  • B.Sc., Quantity Surveying, University of Lagos, Nigeria, 2010
Location: 113 Hardaway Hall ​
Olugbenro is currently working to "Enhance Quality Management in Highway Construction using Emerging Methods". To achieve this he is analyzing and statistically comparing the effects of daytime and nighttime paving operations on the quality of highway construction. This is base knowledge for the evaluation of the use of existing/emerging nondestructive testing technologies for quality management processes/procedures, and the assessment of automation readiness for components of quality assurance/quality control in highway construction.  As an instructor on campus, he is making full use of the Geotechnical and Materials Testing Laboratories.
